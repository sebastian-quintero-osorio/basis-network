\section{Results}

\subsection{Unit and Scenario Test Validation}

All 8 unit tests and 6 scenario tests pass. The unit tests validate FIFO ordering
correctness, capacity enforcement, forced inclusion deadline semantics, and concurrent
access safety. The scenario tests exercise the sequencer under realistic workload
patterns.

\subsection{Block Production Scaling}

Table~\ref{tab:block-scaling} presents block production latency across transaction
counts from 10 to 5{,}000 per block, measured over 30 repetitions each with direct
timing (no ticker jitter).

\begin{table}[htbp]

\label{tab:block-scaling}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{rrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{TX/Block} & \textbf{Avg ($\mu$s)} & \textbf{Min ($\mu$s)} & \textbf{Max ($\mu$s)} & \textbf{Std ($\mu$s)} \\
\midrule
10 & 0.0 & 0.0 & 0.0 & 0.0 \\
50 & 2.7 & 0.0 & 80.0 & 14.6 \\
100 & 19.2 & 0.0 & 503.0 & 92.3 \\
200 & 50.2 & 0.0 & 1{,}507 & 275.1 \\
500 & 140.8 & 0.0 & 1{,}505 & 370.6 \\

\bottomrule

\end{table}

Block production latency scales approximately linearly with transaction count: from
sub-microsecond at 10~TX to $0.885$~ms at 5{,}000~TX. Even at the maximum tested
load of 5{,}000~transactions per block, the sequencer overhead is $<$1~ms,
leaving $>$999~ms of the 1-second block interval budget for EVM execution.

Note: the Min values of 0.0~$\mu$s reflect Windows timer granularity
(${\sim}$15~ms resolution for \texttt{time.Now()}), not actual zero-time operations.
The Go native benchmarks (Table~\ref{tab:go-benchmarks}) provide nanosecond-precision
measurements that resolve this limitation.

A single mempool insertion completes in 338--365~nanoseconds with 3 allocations

block requires ${\sim}$6.5~$\mu$s (7 allocations), enabling ${\sim}$152{,}000
blocks per second---far exceeding the 1~block/second target.

validating the sequence number-based ordering mechanism.

Our block production latency (0.019~ms at 100~tx) is significantly faster than the

and consistent: the published figures include transaction execution, which our
prototype explicitly excludes to isolate sequencer-specific performance.
